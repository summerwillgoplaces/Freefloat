? What am I looking at

Float

Shares actually available to trade publicly. Low float (<20M, green) means small supply — when demand spikes, price can move violently. If a company doesn't publish float, we show total shares outstanding marked SO.

RVOL (relative volume)

Today's volume ÷ 3-month average. 2x+ means unusual interest; the higher, the more eyes on it.

Rotation

Today's volume ÷ float — how many times the entire float has changed hands today. Above 1x is extreme churn.

Gap %

Today's open vs. yesterday's close. Enable Gap mode in the filter bar to see and sort by it — useful pre-market and at the open.

⛔ HALTED (with countdown)

Real halt data from Nasdaq's official trade-halt feed, refreshed every minute. The countdown shows minutes until trading resumes when the exchange has announced a resumption time. LULD volatility pauses are a minimum of 5 minutes but can extend repeatedly if the price is still moving at re-open — so "resume TBD" or a countdown that resets is normal. News-pending halts (T1) have no set length at all.

⚠ HALT?

Our own heuristic flag (big % move + heavy RVOL) marking stocks that might get halted soon. Different from ⛔, which is official.

Trading around halts — what the evidence actually says

First, the mechanics: you cannot buy or sell during a halt. Orders you place may queue for the re-open or be rejected, depending on your broker, and you can cancel resting orders during most halts. When trading resumes, the price often gaps — it re-opens away from the halt price, sometimes dramatically, so stop-losses give no protection through a halt.

What academic research finds: studies of trading halts and LULD pauses consistently show that volatility and bid-ask spreads are elevated immediately after resumption, not calmed — the halt concentrates uncertainty at the re-open. Post-halt direction is close to a coin flip in aggregate: momentum sometimes continues, sometimes sharply reverses, and no published research demonstrates a reliable, durable edge from simply buying or selling every resumption. Halts triggered by pending news resolve on the news itself, which is unknowable in advance by definition.

Risk-management principles that follow from the evidence (education, not a recommendation): decide your exit and invalidation levels before a halt-prone stock halts, not during; assume the re-open can gap against you by more than your stop distance and size the position so that outcome is survivable; prefer limit orders over market orders at resumption, when spreads are widest and fills are worst; and treat repeated back-to-back halts as rising, not falling, risk. If you're unsure what to do with a halted position, that uncertainty is itself information — consult a licensed financial advisor for decisions about your money.
